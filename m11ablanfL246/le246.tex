	\noindent {\bf task 1. a)}\\
	The function may not be differentiable at the point where the expression between the absolute lines becomes zero, i.e. at $x=1$.
	
	\noindent {\bf task 1. b)}\\
	The following applies:
	$$f(x)= \begin{cases} {1 \over (x-1) +1 } = {1\over x} & x \ge 1 \\
		{1 \over -(x-1) + 1 } = { 1 \over 2 -x } & x <1
	\end{cases}
	$$
	$$
	\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{2}
	\begin{array}{lrcl}
		\rm\underline{Case \quad 1.\quad x \ge 1}\\
		& \lim\limits_{x \to 1}\frac{{1\over x} - {1 \over 1}}{x-1}&=&
		\lim\limits_{x \to 1}\frac{{1-x \over x}}{x-1} \\
		& =\lim\limits_{x \to 1}-{1\over x}&=& -1
	\end{array}
	$$
	$$
	\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{2}
	\begin{array}{lrcl}
		\rm\underline{Case\quad  2.\quad x < 1}\\
		&\lim\limits_{x \to 1}\frac{{1\over 2-x} - {1 \over 2-1}}{x-1}&=&
		\lim\limits_{x \to 1}\frac{{1-2+x \over 2-x}}{x-1} \\
		&=\lim\limits_{x \to 1}{1\over 2-x}&=& 1
	\end{array}
	$$
	The left-hand value of the difference quotient does not agree with the right-hand one. It follows that the function is not differentiable at the point $x=1$.
