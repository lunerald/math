	{\bf Training sheet spherical volume }\\
	\begin{enumerate}[1.]
		
		%Task 1
		{\bf \item Can a Styrofoam ball with a radius of $\SI{1}{\meter}$ be carried alone?} Calculate the mass assuming that $\SI{1}{\deci\meter}^ 3$ Styrofoam $\SI{30}{\gram}$ weighs.
		
		%Exercise 2
		{\bf \item Calculate the diameter of a sphere with the following volume:} \\
		a) $V=\SI{1}{\meter}^3$ \hspace{30 pt} b) $V=\SI{49}{\centi \meter}^3$
		
		%Task 3
		\begin{minipage}{0.75\textwidth}
			{\bf \item Three tennis balls are in a cylindrical package.} The can has the same diameter $d$ as a ball. What percentage of the volume is accounted for by the spaces?
		\end{minipage}
		\hfill
		\begin{minipage}{0.2\textwidth}
			\includegraphics[width=4cm]{tendos217}
		\end{minipage}
		
		%Task 4
		{\bf \item A sphere, a cylinder and the base circle of a cone have the same radius.} Calculate the height of the cylinder and the cone so that all three bodies have the same volume.
		
		%Task 5
		{\bf \item An (almost spherical) drop with a diameter of $\SI{4}{\milli\meter}$ falls from a dripping tap every 2 seconds.} How many liters of water are lost over the course of a week?
		
		%Task 6
		{\bf \item The radius of a spherical balloon increases by 10\% as it rises higher.} By what percentage does the volume increase?
		
		%Task 7
		{\bf \item How large is the proportion of the spherical volume of an inscribed sphere to the volume of the cube (in \%)?}
		
		%Task 8
		\begin{minipage}{0.7\textwidth}
			{\bf \item The amount of water on Earth is approximately $ \SI{1.4e9}{\kilo \meter^3}$.}
			\begin{enumerate}[a)]
				\item How deep would the global ocean be if the entire earth's surface were flat ($r_{Earth}=\SI{6370}{\kilo\meter}$)?
				\item Jupiter's moon Europa ($r=\SI{1560}{\kilo\meter}$) is slightly smaller than Earth's moon, but contains around twice the amount of water as Earth in its outer shell; see picture (source: Wikipedia). How deep is the assumed ocean there, including the ice crust?
			\end{enumerate}
		\end{minipage}
		\hspace{0.5 cm}
		\begin{minipage}{0.3\textwidth}
			\includegraphics[width=4cm]{euro217}
		\end{minipage}
		
		
		{\bf \item Task}
		
		\begin{enumerate}[a)]
			\item Two metal balls (radius $r=1.5~a$) are melted down and a new ball is cast. Calculate the radius of the new sphere. (Substitute result: $2~a$)
			\item How does the surface area of the new sphere relate to the total surface area of the two original spheres? Enter the result in percent.
		\end{enumerate}
		
		%Exercise 2
		{\bf \item An orange with an outer diameter of $\SI{8}{\centi\meter}$ and a peel thickness of $\SI{6}{\milli\meter}$ is to be squeezed.} Assume that that $70\%$ of the pulp can be converted into juice. How many liters of juice do 5 oranges like this make?
		
		
		%Task
		\begin{minipage}{0.7\textwidth}
			{\bf \item Can a golf ball float? A golf ball has a mass of 45.9 g and a diameter of 42.7 mm.}
			\begin{enumerate}[a)]
				\item Calculate the volume and surface area of this nearly spherical golf ball.
				\item Use the volume from a) to calculate the density of the ball and compare it with the density of water.
				\item Does the golf ball float (if missed) in the lake of a golf course or does it sink?
			\end{enumerate}
		\end{minipage}
